## What Is PPI?

PPI stands for **pixels per inch** — it's the measure of pixel density on a digital display. Unlike DPI (dots per inch), which refers to print resolution, PPI tells you how many individual pixels are packed into every inch of your screen.

A 24" monitor at 1920 × 1080 has roughly 92 PPI. A 6.1" iPhone 15 display at 2556 × 1179 has 460 PPI. The same inch of physical space contains five times more pixels on the phone than on the monitor.

## Why PPI Breaks Naive Rulers

Most online rulers make a dangerous assumption: that your screen has **96 PPI**. This was a reasonable default for older Windows monitors, but modern displays range from about 92 PPI (standard monitors) to over 500 PPI (flagship smartphones).

Here's the problem in concrete terms:

| Device | Actual PPI | Ruler at 96 PPI (6" object) | Measured Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| 24" 1080p monitor | 92 | Uses 96 PPI | 5.75" (under) |
| 27" 4K monitor | 163 | Uses 96 PPI | 10.2" (over) |
| iPhone 15 Pro | 460 | Uses 96 PPI | 28.75" (wildly over) |

A ruler tool that ignores PPI can be off by **hundreds of percent** on modern high-resolution displays.

## The Zoom Factor

Browser zoom compounds the PPI problem. At 150% zoom, a 96 PPI setting effectively becomes 144 PPI in terms of rendered size. That's why RulerKit displays a zoom warning whenever your browser is not at 100% — it's one of the most common sources of measurement error.

## Real-World Impact

### For Designers

A UI designer measuring a 400px button needs to know how large that button will appear on different screens. At 96 PPI, 400px = 4.17". At 163 PPI (a common 4K monitor), 400px = 2.45". Without PPI awareness, a button meant to be comfortably tappable could end up tiny.

### For DIY and Home Use

If you're using RulerKit to measure a gap for a replacement part, a miscalibrated PPI could mean ordering the wrong size. A part you measure as 5" on a misconfigured screen might really be 4.5" — and it won't fit.

### For Students

Online homework often requires measurements. A student using a non-calibrated online ruler might submit incorrect answers through no fault of their own — the tool itself was lying to them.

## Checking Your Screen's PPI

You can look up your monitor's specifications or use RulerKit's auto-detection feature. For the most accurate results, physically measure your screen's viewable diagonal and compute PPI with the formula:

```
PPI = √(width_px² + height_px²) / diagonal_inches
```

Alternatively, grab a credit card and use our reference-based calibration — it takes 10 seconds and requires no math.
