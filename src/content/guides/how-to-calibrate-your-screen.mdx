## Why Screen Calibration Matters

An online ruler is only as accurate as your screen calibration. Unlike a physical ruler, a digital ruler relies on knowing the exact pixel density (PPI) of your display. If your screen's PPI is misconfigured, every measurement you take with our tool will be slightly off — and that margin of error compounds the longer the object you're measuring.

Whether you're a designer checking print dimensions, a student completing a homework assignment, or a DIY hobbyist measuring a replacement part, calibration ensures the numbers you see on screen match the real world.

## Step 1: Use a Reference Object

Before adjusting anything, grab a physical reference object with a known length. The best candidates are:

- A standard **credit card** (85.60 mm × 53.98 mm / 3.370 in × 2.125 in)
- A **US dollar bill** (156 mm / 6.14 in wide)
- An **A4 sheet of paper** (210 mm × 297 mm / 8.27 in × 11.69 in)

Place the object flat against your screen and note how it aligns with the default ruler on RulerKit.

## Step 2: Adjust Your Display PPI Setting

RulerKit lets you fine-tune the PPI value used for rendering measurements. Here's how to find the correct PPI for your setup:

### Find Your Monitor's Native Resolution and Diagonal Size

```
PPI = √(horizontal_pixels² + vertical_pixels²) / diagonal_inches
```

For example, a 27" monitor at 2560 × 1440:

```
PPI = √(2560² + 1440²) / 27 ≈ 108.8 PPI
```

Enter this value into RulerKit's calibration settings.

### For Laptops and Phones

Most modern laptops and phones have very high DPI displays. Check your device's technical specifications online — manufacturers almost always list the PPI. Common values:

- **MacBook Pro 14" (2023)**: 254 PPI
- **MacBook Air 13" (M2)**: 227 PPI
- **iPhone 15 Pro Max**: 460 PPI
- **Samsung Galaxy S24 Ultra**: 505 PPI
- **Generic 1080p 24" monitor**: 92 PPI

## Step 3: Verify With the Reference Object

After setting your PPI, re-check the ruler against your reference object. The on-screen measurement should match the real-world length within ±1 mm.

If it's still off, fine-tune the PPI value incrementally:

- If the ruler reads **too long**, increase the PPI
- If the ruler reads **too short**, decrease the PPI

Adjust by 2–5 PPI at a time and re-check until your reference object lines up perfectly.

## Step 4: Save Your Calibration Profile

RulerKit can store your calibrated PPI in a browser cookie or localStorage entry. Once saved, you won't need to recalibrate unless you switch displays or change your screen resolution.

## Troubleshooting Common Issues

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ruler is consistently short | PPI set too high | Lower PPI by 5–10 |
| Ruler is consistently long | PPI set too low | Raise PPI by 5–10 |
| Accuracy changes when I zoom | Browser zoom interferes | Reset zoom to 100% |
| Mobile ruler is inaccurate | OS display scaling active | Check device PPI specsheet |

## Browser Zoom and Scaling

Always ensure your browser zoom is set to **100%** when using RulerKit. Zooming in or out effectively changes the perceived PPI and will throw off measurements. You can reset zoom quickly with `Ctrl+0` (Windows/Linux) or `Cmd+0` (macOS).

Similarly, operating system display scaling (e.g., Windows "Scale & Layout" set to 125% or 150%) needs to be factored in. On Windows, divide the native PPI by the scaling factor — for example, `108 PPI × 1.25 = 135 PPI` at 125% scaling.

## Final Check

Once calibrated, try measuring a few different objects around your desk — a pen, a phone, a notebook — against the physical ruler. If all readings are within tolerance, your calibration is complete. For best results, repeat this process whenever you use a different monitor or change your display settings.
